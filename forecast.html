Landing · Thesis

Prediction Replaces Uncertainty

Before the first customer walks into a Melbourne supermarket in 2030, the most consequential decision of the morning has already been made — not by a manager, buyer or supplier, but by a forecasting algorithm that knows the store better than anyone who works inside it.

That system has synthesised last week's weather, tomorrow's school holiday, a local sports event, and the purchasing history of 2.3 million loyalty cardholders. The shelves are already stocked accordingly. The casual roster is already optimised. The markdown on the yoghurt expiring tonight has been calculated. Nothing looks unusual. The store still smells like bread and cardboard. But underneath the ordinary morning, the economics of retail has changed in a way that most policy frameworks were not designed to address.

This is the world the prediction rent makes: more efficient on the surface, more unequal beneath — unless deliberate policy choices intervene before the infrastructure is locked in.

"AI will not simply automate Australian retail. It will turn uncertainty into prediction — and prediction into a new form of market power that compounds at the top of an already concentrated sector."

The central economic argument of this forecast is that AI creates a prediction rent: the surplus value captured by whoever builds the most accurate forecasting infrastructure in a thin-margin, high-volume, data-rich industry.

This matters because retail is not a marginal industry. It is a mass employer, a daily household service, and a politically sensitive sector connected to food prices, part-time work and consumer trust. The question this forecast addresses is not whether AI reshapes retail — it will — but who captures the gains, who absorbs the disruption, and what policy choices can shift those outcomes before the structural advantage becomes self-reinforcing.

Jobs and Skills Australia (2026) reports that the retail sector employs approximately 1.34 million Australians — 9.1% of total employment — with a 50% part-time workforce share, making distributional questions central to any honest forecast. Jobs and Skills Australia 2026, Retail Trade Industry Profile, Australian Government, Canberra.

The retail sector's size, its labour-market profile, and its existing concentration make it an ideal lens through which to examine AI's broader economic consequences — and a compelling test case for what equitable technology governance can look like in practice.

The retail sector's "pincer movement" of rising costs and weakening demand is precisely the economic context that makes AI investment rational, not aspirational.

Retailers under margin pressure do not invest in AI because it appears futuristic. They invest because it promises a specific economic exchange: capital expenditure today for reduced operational uncertainty tomorrow. Where the sector cannot grow its way to profitability, it will predict its way there.

Deloitte Access Economics (2026) forecasts retail turnover growth of 1.8% in 2026, down from 2.3% in 2025. ABS data (2026a) confirms household spending fell 1.1% month-on-month in April 2026, with broad-based weakness across food, clothing and transport. Deloitte Access Economics 2026; ABS 2026a, Monthly Household Spending Indicator, April 2026.

The prediction rent emerges not in a boom market but in a contested one — making its distributional consequences more acute, not less.

Economic Mechanisms

Three Channels of Transformation

AI reshapes retail not through a single dramatic rupture but through three interlocking mechanisms, each reinforcing the prediction rent from a different direction: the operational layer, the personalisation layer, and the labour market.

01
Channel One

The Operational Prediction Layer

The first and least visible transformation is operational: AI substitutes for uncertainty across the entire supply chain, not merely for specific workers at specific tasks.

Retail looks simple from the customer side — a product is either on the shelf or it is not. Behind that moment is a chain of forecasting, ordering, logistics and staffing decisions, each carrying a cost when it goes wrong. Overstock produces waste. Understock produces lost sales and customer defection. Roster too few workers and service collapses; roster too many and labour costs erode already-thin margins. In food retail, where the ACCC (2025a) found Woolworths and Coles operating with gross margins of approximately 25–27%, the compounding gains from tighter prediction are substantial at scale.

McKinsey & Company (2023) estimates that AI-driven demand forecasting can reduce inventory costs by 15–20% and improve forecast accuracy by 10–40% across retail operations — representing potential savings in the hundreds of millions annually on a sector turnover base exceeding AU$380 billion. The Productivity Commission (2025) identifies AI as a meaningful driver of productivity in data-rich sectors, but notes gains are conditional on data quality, firm capability and regulatory clarity. McKinsey & Company 2023, The Economic Potential of Generative AI; Productivity Commission 2025, Inquiry Report No. 111.

The prediction rent begins here — in logistics dashboards and shelf-monitoring systems most customers will never see — and it begins accumulating asymmetrically in favour of firms with the most data and the most sophisticated models.

The deeper economic point is that operational AI is not primarily a technology substituting for labour — it is a technology substituting for uncertainty itself, and the competitive moat this creates is not replicable by effort alone.

When a system can predict that a Footscray supermarket needs 15% more pasta on a Tuesday before a school holiday — because it has observed that pattern across 47 comparable stores and 300 similar events — the informational advantage does not merely produce efficiency. It produces a self-reinforcing edge: each correct forecast improves the training data for the next, compounding an advantage that smaller retailers, without comparable data volumes, cannot close by working harder or by following market signals after the fact.

This self-reinforcing dynamic is what transforms an operational efficiency into a structural rent — and it is why the distributional implications of AI in retail cannot be addressed by technology policy alone.

02
Channel Two

The Personalised Shelf and the Problem of Information Power

By 2030, the most strategically important shelf in Australian retail may not be physical — it may be the personalised digital feed that each customer encounters before they ever enter a store or open an app.

Loyalty apps, online carts, search histories and payment data already generate a detailed portrait of consumer behaviour. AI makes this data economically active by predicting what each customer might buy, when they are likely to switch brands, and what discount, reminder or substitute is most likely to retain them. In Australian grocery retail, where Coles Flybuys and Woolworths Everyday Rewards collectively hold behavioural data on tens of millions of households, personalisation is not a future possibility — it is a current commercial practice being dramatically accelerated by AI.

McKinsey & Company (2023) reports that AI-driven personalisation delivers measurable revenue uplifts of 10–15% in retail settings. The ACCC (2025a) found that promotional practices in the supermarket sector were already opaque, with headline discounts routinely misleading consumers about genuine price reductions, and noted that loyalty programs created information asymmetries that disadvantaged less engaged consumers. McKinsey 2023; ACCC 2025a, Supermarkets Inquiry: Final Report.

Personalisation as a channel of the prediction rent is ethically complex precisely because its efficiency gains and its distributional risks are inseparable: the same capability that makes shopping more convenient also makes pricing less transparent.

The economic issue is not whether personalisation improves the customer experience — often it does — but whether AI allows retailers to know consumers better than consumers understand the pricing system they are navigating.

Hayek (1945) argued that the market's genius lies in its capacity to aggregate dispersed knowledge through prices. AI-enabled personalisation partially inverts this logic: the retailer now holds more information about the individual consumer's preferences, price sensitivity and switching likelihood than the price mechanism itself reveals. Two customers walking through the same digital store may face different prices, different offers and different assumptions about their willingness to pay — an outcome structurally analogous to third-degree price discrimination, now executed at algorithmic scale and speed.

The ACCC (2025b) warns in its Digital Platform Services Inquiry that data-driven market power creates structural barriers to competition that existing regulatory frameworks — designed for observable price conduct — were not built to address. ACCC 2025b, Digital Platform Services Inquiry: Final Report.

The prediction rent in personalisation is therefore a rent extracted from information asymmetry — and unless policy addresses the asymmetry directly, convenience and exploitation will remain difficult to distinguish at scale.

03
Channel Three

Work Is Redesigned, Not Erased

The common forecast says AI will "take jobs"; a more rigorous forecast — grounded in the task-based model of Autor (2015) and Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) — says AI will restructure tasks, shift bargaining values and change what retail workers are hired to do.

Retail jobs are not monolithic — they are bundles of tasks: scanning items, answering questions, restocking shelves, calming frustrated customers, checking inventory, following promotions and coordinating teams. AI can substitute for some tasks (routine information-processing, basic stock queries, standard promotional content) while complementing others (exception management, human service recovery, system supervision). The cashier role is not abolished overnight; it is progressively redesigned around AI-assisted workflows, while self-checkouts and digital interfaces absorb the routine scanning work that was never, in economic terms, the most valuable part of the role.

Jobs and Skills Australia (2025) finds that current generative AI technologies are more likely to augment work than fully automate it, though routine clerical and transactional roles face measurably higher displacement risk. The RBA (2025) similarly finds that Australian firms anticipate AI to be productivity-enhancing while transforming the skills mix they require — with long adjustment periods that can impose transitional costs on workers even when the aggregate outcome is positive. JSA 2025, Our Gen AI Transition: Implications for Work and Skills; RBA 2025, Technology Investment and AI, Bulletin November.

The task redesign channel connects the prediction rent to labour outcomes: if productivity gains from AI flow to shareholders and data-rich platforms rather than to retail workers, the sector becomes simultaneously more efficient and more unequal — a structural change hidden behind stable employment headlines.

The absence of mass unemployment is not evidence that distributional outcomes are benign — it may simply mean that labour market harm takes a slower, less visible form.

Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2018) displacement-versus-productivity-effect framework is critical here. Automation creates a displacement effect when machines substitute for specific tasks — but this can be offset by a productivity effect (growing firms hire more workers in new roles) and the creation of new tasks entirely. The question is whether the new roles are accessible to the same workers whose old roles were displaced — particularly in a sector characterised by casualisation, migrant labour, and limited upskilling infrastructure. Without deliberate policy, task redesign may produce a retail workforce that is smaller and more skilled at the top, and more precarious at the bottom.

This is why the skills compact described in the Policy Playbook is not merely a training measure — it is the mechanism through which the labour channel of the prediction rent is either addressed or left to deepen.

The baseline scenario is quiet automation: AI spreads across Australian retail, but mostly beneath the surface, and its gains accumulate at the firms that already hold the most data.

Large retailers use AI for stock forecasting, rostering, shelf monitoring and personalised offers. Productivity improves but the efficiency gains concentrate at the top of the market. Smaller retailers access AI only through third-party platforms they do not own or control, deepening platform dependency rather than building independent capability. Employment does not collapse, but role composition shifts quietly: entry-level transactional tasks shrink, hybrid digital-service roles grow, and workers caught between those transitions receive little formal support for the adjustment.

The RBA (2025) notes that productivity gains from technology often take time to materialise because firms must change workflows, train staff and adjust management practices — and this adjustment period is borne unevenly, with large firms adapting faster while SMEs and casual workers absorb the friction. RBA 2025, Technology Investment and AI, Bulletin November.

In the baseline scenario, the prediction rent accrues to incumbents — the store looks the same, but the power structure behind it does not.

Policy Inputs
Consumer Data Right extended to retail; JSA skills compact funded via TAFE; ACCC gains algorithmic pricing monitoring powers; targeted SME AI adoption grants introduced.
Productivity Outcome
Index 100 → 132 by 2030 — strong gains, broadly distributed across firm sizes and worker categories.

The upside scenario is shared productivity: AI adoption is supported by practical training, accessible data infrastructure, transparent pricing rules and targeted support for smaller retailers — making the prediction rent a sector-wide productivity gain rather than a corporate moat.

A regional grocer accesses affordable demand-forecasting tools without surrendering customer data to a major platform. A small fashion retailer personalises customer communications with tools she controls. Workers in large retailers receive practical training in AI-assisted inventory management and customer data rights, rather than simply being supervised by algorithmic rosters they cannot interrogate or challenge. The prediction rent is not eliminated — Woolworths and Coles still hold scale advantages — but its concentration is moderated by data access, skills investment and competitive oversight that functions in real time.

Jobs and Skills Australia (2025) argues that adaptation, lifelong learning and accessible training will be essential as generative AI changes skills demand across the economy. The Productivity Commission (2025) supports an outcomes-based regulatory approach that enables productivity while managing concentration risk, emphasising that data access policy is as important as technology investment policy. JSA 2025, Our Gen AI Transition; Productivity Commission 2025, Inquiry Report No. 111.

Shared productivity requires institutional design — it will not happen automatically because AI tools become cheaper; without deliberate investment, the upside scenario remains technically available but practically inaccessible to the firms and workers who need it most.

Policy Inputs

Productivity Outcome

The downside scenario is algorithmic concentration: AI deepens the market power of retailers that already dominate the sector, making retail more efficient at the aggregate level while making competition less visible and consumer harm harder to detect or challenge.

The ACCC (2025a) already warns that Australia's grocery sector is oligopolistic with limited incentives for aggressive price competition. In this scenario, AI deepens that structure: Woolworths and Coles use superior first-party data to train stronger personalisation models, negotiate with suppliers from greater informational advantage, and implement dynamic pricing that regulatory agencies — equipped with tools designed for observable price collusion — cannot easily detect or challenge. The ACCC's (2025b) concern about digital platform market power becomes a retail concern: AI-enabled concentration is structurally invisible because its mechanism is prediction and personalisation rather than explicit price coordination.

Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) warn that productivity gains from automation do not automatically translate into proportional wage or labour benefits — and can instead concentrate surplus in capital, particularly when labour has limited bargaining power and regulatory oversight is slow to adapt. Acemoglu & Restrepo 2018, Artificial Intelligence, Automation and Work, NBER Working Paper No. 24196.

In the downside scenario, the prediction rent becomes a structural barrier to competition — retail efficiency rises while retail equity falls, and the gap between the two is hidden inside algorithms that neither consumers nor regulators can easily read.

Policy Playbook

Making the Forecast Better

Three targeted interventions — chosen because they address the prediction rent directly, rather than AI in the abstract — can shift the trajectory from baseline toward the upside. Each policy names a mechanism, not a goal.

Policy 01 · Skills

Retail AI Skills Compact

A sector-specific, TAFE-delivered program in AI-assisted stock management, algorithmic roster interpretation, customer data rights and AI system supervision. Not a generic digital literacy campaign — practical tools for the shop floor, accessible to casual and migrant workers, not just head office.

With 50% part-time employment and high casual workforce shares, training that only reaches corporate headquarters misses the workers who will absorb the greatest task disruption. The policy must be designed for the workforce that actually exists.

TAFE delivery Casual-accessible JSA partnership
Policy 02 · Data Rights

Extend Consumer Data Right to Retail

The current CDR framework covers banking and energy. Retail data — loyalty programs, purchase history, personalisation profiles — is equally powerful and equally unportable. Extension would allow consumers to understand, correct and share their own retail data, directly reducing the information asymmetry that makes personalisation powerful for retailers and opaque for customers.

A consumer who can access their own data — and choose where it goes — changes the bargaining structure without banning personalisation itself.

CDR extension Consumer portability ACCC oversight
Policy 03 · Competition

Algorithmic Pricing Oversight

Equip the ACCC with the mandate and technical capability to monitor algorithmic pricing conduct and personalised promotional practices. The Supermarkets Inquiry revealed that price signals were already difficult to interpret; AI-driven dynamic pricing will make this vastly harder without targeted regulatory capability.

An outcomes-based approach — the Productivity Commission's (2025) preferred model — means monitoring competitive conduct and consumer outcomes, not attempting to proscribe specific technologies.

ACCC mandate Outcomes-based SME protection

Limitations & Uncertainty

Productivity estimates draw from global McKinsey figures and may overstate gains in Australia's relatively concentrated, lower-productivity retail market. Scenario index values are illustrative of direction and magnitude, not precise projections. Three structural uncertainties apply: faster AI capability diffusion, slower regulatory adaptation, and distributional effects diverging from historical technology transitions.

Conclusion

The Checkout Is a Choice

By 2030, the most powerful force in Australian retail will be invisible — embedded in forecast models, loyalty algorithms, dynamic rosters and personalised digital shelves that most customers will never directly see or interrogate.

The visible store may still feel familiar. The decision architecture behind it will not. The prediction rent is real and it is growing: each dataset accumulated, each forecast improved, each personalisation refined adds to an informational advantage that compounds at the top of an already concentrated market. The question is not whether AI reshapes Australian retail — it will, and the process has already begun. The question is whether the rules, institutions and investments Australia puts in place before 2030 ensure that the productivity gains from prediction are distributed across 1.34 million workers, millions of consumers and thousands of small businesses — or whether they accumulate quietly in the balance sheets of two companies that already dominate the sector.

The three policy interventions identified in this forecast — a retail AI skills compact, extension of the Consumer Data Right to retail, and algorithmic pricing oversight for the ACCC — are not radical proposals. They are the minimum institutional response required to shift the trajectory from the baseline scenario to the upside. They cost less than the distributional harm of getting this wrong. Productivity Commission 2025; JSA 2025; ACCC 2025a, 2025b.

The technology is arriving regardless. The future checkout is not a machine — it is a choice about what kind of economy Australia builds around one.
