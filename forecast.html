Landing · Thesis

What Actually Changes

Think about the last time you bought milk. There was a price on the shelf. You could compare it with the price next door. That shared, observable price is one of the oldest mechanisms in market economics — and by 2030, it may no longer meaningfully exist in Australian retail.

What replaces it is not cheaper milk or better service. What replaces it is a system that knows, before you've even entered the store or opened the app, what price you'll accept, which promotion will change your behaviour, and how likely you are to switch brands if the deal isn't good enough. For you, that might feel like convenience. For the retailer, it's an entirely new form of market power — one that scales with data, compounds with prediction, and currently sits almost entirely outside the reach of Australia's regulatory frameworks.

The ACCC's 2025 Supermarkets Inquiry Final Report found that Woolworths and Coles operate in an oligopolistic market structure with limited incentives for aggressive price competition. Promotional pricing practices — including was-now pricing — were found to be systematically misleading. AI personalisation will make these dynamics significantly harder to observe and challenge. ACCC 2025a, Supermarkets Inquiry: Final Report, Australian Competition and Consumer Commission, Canberra.

The central forecast of this essay is conditional: AI could create a more productive and responsive retail economy, but without deliberate policy, it will almost certainly deepen the market power of the retailers and platforms that already hold the most data.

"The most consequential transformation in Australian retail won't be visible on the shop floor. It will happen in the systems behind it — and the question is who those systems serve."
The Hayek Inversion: When the Retailer Knows More Than the Market

Friedrich Hayek's (1945) celebrated argument for market economies rested on a single insight: no central planner could ever aggregate the dispersed, local knowledge that millions of independent buyers and sellers communicate through prices. The price system was, in Hayek's framing, an information miracle — decentralised, spontaneous, impossible to replicate.

AI in retail performs something like a Hayek inversion. When Woolworths' loyalty algorithm analyses your purchase history across 1,200 weekly shops, it holds more structured knowledge about your preferences, habits and price sensitivity than you consciously hold about yourself. The information asymmetry Hayek thought impossible to centralise is, in a personalised retail context, being centralised — by the retailer. That's not a minor efficiency gain. That's a structural shift in who holds economic power in the relationship between buyer and seller.

Retail is not a marginal sector in Australia — it's a mass employer, a daily household service, and a politically sensitive industry connected to food prices, part-time work, and consumer trust.

Jobs and Skills Australia (2026) reports that retail employs approximately 1.34 million Australians — 9.1% of total employment — with a 50% part-time workforce share. This is not a sector on the periphery of the AI debate. It is the AI debate's most immediate lived consequence for the broadest cross-section of Australian workers. Jobs and Skills Australia 2026, Retail Trade Industry Profile, Australian Government, Canberra.

The prediction rent, once established, doesn't stay in the supply chain or the loyalty app — it flows through labour markets, competition dynamics, and consumer welfare. That's why this is an economics story, not just a technology story.

The pressure is the point: AI investment in retail is not a luxury experiment — it's a margin-protection strategy in a sector that cannot afford to keep guessing.

Where the sector can't grow its way to profitability, it will predict its way there. Fewer stockouts, tighter rosters, less promotional waste — each one is a small efficiency gain that compounds across hundreds of stores and millions of transactions. The firm that automates uncertainty first doesn't just become more efficient; it starts to pull away from competitors who are still paying the cost of being wrong.

ABS data (2026a) shows household spending fell 1.1% month-on-month in April 2026, with falls in food, clothing, footwear and transport. Deloitte Access Economics (2026) describes this as a structural "pincer movement" — rising input costs meeting weakening consumer demand — that makes operational efficiency not optional but existential for mid-tier retailers. ABS 2026a, Monthly Household Spending Indicator, April 2026; Deloitte Access Economics 2026.

This is the economic soil in which the prediction rent grows: stress, not innovation, is the primary driver of AI adoption — and that matters for how we think about who benefits and who gets left behind.

Economic Mechanisms

Three Channels, One Thesis

AI enters retail through three distinct economic channels. Each one reinforces the prediction rent from a different angle. Scroll through them below — the chart on the right updates to show the data behind each argument.

01
Channel One

The Operational Layer: Substituting for Uncertainty

Start with the back room, not the shop floor. The most significant AI-driven changes in retail won't be the ones customers see — they'll happen in demand forecasting, inventory ordering and shift scheduling, running quietly in systems nobody directly observes.

Retail looks simple from a customer's perspective: the product is either on the shelf or it isn't. Behind that moment is a chain of hundreds of decisions — how much to order, when to order it, how to move it from warehouse to shelf, how many staff to roster on a Tuesday that turns out to be unexpectedly busy. Get any of those decisions wrong at scale and you lose money. AI attacks this problem not by replacing specific workers, but by compressing the gap between what a store expects to happen and what actually happens. Each percentage point improvement in forecast accuracy eliminates waste that previously someone had to absorb.

McKinsey & Company (2023) estimates that AI-driven demand forecasting can reduce retail inventory costs by 15–20% and improve forecast accuracy by 10–40%. The Productivity Commission (2025) identifies AI as a meaningful productivity driver in data-rich sectors, conditional on data quality, firm capability and regulatory clarity. McKinsey & Company 2023, The Economic Potential of Generative AI; Productivity Commission 2025, Inquiry Report No. 111.

The prediction rent begins here — in logistics dashboards that customers never see — and it begins accumulating asymmetrically in favour of firms that already have the most operational data to train on.

The Compounding Edge: Why Data Advantages Don't Close

Here is what makes operational AI different from most technology investments: the advantage compounds. A better forecast today generates better training data for tomorrow's forecast. A retailer with five years of granular sales data — by store, by hour, by weather condition, by local event — doesn't just have more data than a smaller competitor. They have a model that has been tested and refined against real outcomes at a scale that can't be replicated by starting fresh.

This is the structural barrier nobody is talking about. The question isn't whether a small retailer can afford AI forecasting tools — increasingly, affordable tools exist. The question is whether their data is deep enough to make those tools work. In retail, data depth is itself a function of market share. Woolworths and Coles are not just larger. They are structurally positioned to have better models, permanently, unless data portability policy intervenes.

02
Channel Two

The Personalised Shelf: When Convenience Becomes Capture

When you scan your Everyday Rewards card at Woolworths, you're not just earning points. You're providing data that makes a prediction model slightly more accurate — and that accuracy is worth more to Woolworths than the discount it just gave you.

By 2030, the most strategically important shelf in Australian retail may not be physical. It may be the personalised feed in a customer's phone: the offers she sees before she decides which store to visit, the substitutes that appear when her regular brand is out of stock, the reminder that nudges a purchase she hadn't planned. AI makes loyalty data economically active in a new way — not just as a record of past purchases, but as a live predictor of future behaviour that can be acted on in real time. What looks like a helpful service is simultaneously a system of precision influence at scale.

McKinsey & Company (2023) reports that AI-driven personalisation delivers measurable revenue uplifts of 10–15% in retail settings. The ACCC (2025a) found that existing loyalty and promotional practices were already opaque enough to confuse consumers about genuine value. AI personalisation intensifies this: prices, discounts and recommendations may become individualised in ways that make comparison-shopping structurally harder. McKinsey & Company 2023; ACCC 2025a, Supermarkets Inquiry: Final Report.

The prediction rent in personalisation extracts value from information asymmetry: the retailer's model knows your price sensitivity — and will charge accordingly, unless policy addresses that asymmetry directly.

The Corner Store Paradox: Why the Small Retailer Might Actually Win

Here's the counterintuitive case. The assumption in most AI retail forecasts is that scale wins — Woolworths and Coles have the data, they'll have the best models, and everyone else will fall behind. That may be true for national-scale prediction. But it may not be true for hyper-local prediction.

The independent grocer in Newtown knows things that will never appear in a national loyalty dataset: that the Italian family at the end of the street buys three times as much olive oil before Christmas, that Friday evenings mean a run on beer before the local pub fills up, that the construction crew three blocks over buys two dozen Gatorades on hot days. That is dense, relational, community-specific knowledge — and it is, fundamentally, a prediction asset. The challenge is whether small retailers get access to affordable AI tools that let them operationalise what they already know intuitively. If policy creates that access, the corner store's local intelligence becomes a competitive moat that a national algorithm literally cannot replicate.

03
Channel Three

The Labour Channel: Tasks Change Before Jobs Do

The common story says AI will "take jobs." A more accurate story, and a more troubling one in some ways, is that AI will take tasks — and the labour market harm will arrive before the employment statistics catch it.

A retail job isn't one thing. It's a bundle of tasks: scanning items, answering questions, managing stock, handling difficult customers, running promotions, supervising staff. AI is not uniformly good at all of these. It is very good at the routine, information-processing tasks — basic stock queries, standard scheduling, promotional content generation — and much weaker at the interpersonal, contextual, exception-handling tasks that define quality service. This means AI doesn't eliminate the retail worker; it hollows out the routine parts of their role while leaving (or creating) demand for the complex parts. Whether that's good or bad depends entirely on whether the worker gets the training and wages that should accompany the upgrade in role complexity.

Jobs and Skills Australia (2025) finds that current generative AI technologies are more likely to augment work than fully automate it, but that routine clerical and transactional roles face measurably higher displacement risk. The RBA (2025) finds Australian firms expect AI to be productivity-enhancing long-term while transforming the skills mix required — with long transitional periods that impose real costs on workers even when aggregate outcomes are positive. JSA 2025, Our Gen AI Transition: Implications for Work and Skills; RBA 2025, Technology Investment and AI, Bulletin November.

Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) warn that automation's productivity gains do not automatically translate into wage gains — and in retail, with high casual rates and weak collective bargaining, that warning is particularly relevant.

The Algorithmic Roster: A New Form of Precarity That Wage Data Won't Capture

The most underanalysed labour story in AI retail isn't about job loss. It's about scheduling power. By 2030, shift schedules at major retailers may not be set by a manager who knows your name and your situation. They may be generated by an algorithm that optimises for predicted customer flow — and sends you your roster 48 hours out, sometimes 24.

Your ability to plan your week — childcare arrangements, second jobs, family commitments, study — becomes a function of how predictable the store's demand model is. The workers who bear this uncertainty most acutely are casual employees: the 50% part-time, often young, often migrant workforce that retail depends on. Employment data might show stable headcount. It won't show that the planning horizon for a worker's life has been compressed by an algorithm that has no stake in what happens to her outside the store. This is a form of economic harm that current labour statistics are not designed to measure — and that makes it politically invisible.

The baseline isn't catastrophic. It's quietly unfair — and quietly inefficient in ways that only become visible over a longer horizon.

Large retailers adopt AI for forecasting, rostering and personalisation. Productivity improves, but gains concentrate at the top. Smaller retailers can access AI only through third-party platforms they don't control, deepening dependency rather than building capability. Employment doesn't collapse, but role composition shifts quietly: entry-level transactional tasks shrink, hybrid digital-service roles grow, and workers caught in the transition receive little formal support. The algorithm sets the roster. The worker adapts.

The RBA (2025) notes that productivity gains from technology often take time to materialise because firms must change workflows, train staff and adjust management practices — and this adjustment is borne unevenly, with large firms adapting faster while SMEs and casual workers absorb the friction.RBA 2025, Technology Investment and AI, Bulletin November.

In the baseline scenario, the prediction rent accrues to incumbents. The store looks the same; the power structure behind it does not.

Policy Inputs
CDR extended to retail; JSA skills compact funded (~$200M+) via TAFE; ACCC gains algorithmic pricing oversight; SME AI adoption grants introduced.
2030 Outcome
Index ≈ 132 — strong productivity gains broadly distributed across firm sizes and worker categories.

The upside scenario requires institutional design, not technological optimism — it will not happen because AI tools get cheaper.

A regional grocer accesses affordable demand-forecasting tools without surrendering customer data to a platform she doesn't control. A small fashion retailer personalises communications without handing her customer relationships to Woolworths' ecosystem. Workers in large retailers receive practical training in AI-assisted tools rather than simply being managed by them. The prediction rent isn't eliminated — scale advantages remain — but its concentration is moderated by data access, skills investment and competitive oversight that functions in real time, not retrospectively.

Jobs and Skills Australia (2025) argues that adaptation and accessible training are essential for equitable AI transition. The Productivity Commission (2025) supports outcomes-based regulation that enables productivity while managing concentration risk — noting that data access policy is as important as technology investment policy.JSA 2025; Productivity Commission 2025, Inquiry Report No. 111.

Shared productivity is possible. It requires Australia to treat retail AI as a public policy problem, not just a market outcome.

Policy Inputs
Regulatory lag; no data portability; no antitrust response to algorithmic market power; skills investment deferred; algorithmic pricing unchallenged.
2030 Outcome
Index ≈ 112 — efficiency gains offset by market dysfunction, reduced competition and concentrated consumer harm.

The downside scenario is not a dystopia — it's the unremarkable result of leaving an already-concentrated market to self-regulate through a technology transition.

Woolworths and Coles use superior first-party data to train stronger personalisation models, negotiate with suppliers from greater informational advantage, and implement dynamic pricing that regulators — equipped with tools designed for observable price collusion — cannot effectively challenge. The ACCC's concern about digital platform power (2025b) becomes a retail concern: AI-enabled concentration is structurally invisible because its mechanism is prediction, not coordination. Smaller retailers deepen their dependence on platforms they don't control. Workers on algorithmic rosters have less negotiating leverage than workers managed by humans.

Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) warn that automation's productivity gains do not automatically translate into proportional labour benefits — and can concentrate surplus in capital, particularly when labour bargaining power is weak and regulatory response is slow.Acemoglu & Restrepo 2018, NBER Working Paper No. 24196.

The prediction rent becomes a structural barrier to competition — efficiency rises, equity falls, and the gap between the two is hidden inside models that neither consumers nor regulators can read.

Policy Playbook

The Rules That Shape the Outcome

These three policies don't target AI in the abstract. They target the specific mechanisms through which the prediction rent concentrates. Each one names a mechanism, a change, and a tradeoff.

Policy 01 · Labour

Retail AI Skills Compact

Sector-specific, TAFE-delivered training in AI-assisted stock management, algorithmic roster tools, customer data rights and AI supervision skills. Not a generic digital literacy program — practical tools for the workers who will actually interact with these systems.

The key design constraint: with 50% part-time employment and high casual and migrant workforce shares, training that only reaches head office bypasses the workers most vulnerable to algorithmic task redesign.

Tradeoff: Costs public funding. Risk is low uptake if designed for head office rather than shop floor. KPI: participation rates across casual and part-time workers, not just full-time staff.

TAFE deliveryCasual-accessibleJSA partnership
Policy 02 · Data Rights

Extend Consumer Data Right to Retail

The CDR framework currently covers banking and energy. Retail loyalty data — purchase history, inferred preferences, pricing sensitivity — is equally powerful and entirely unportable. Extension gives consumers the right to access, correct and transfer their own retail data.

This directly addresses the Hayek Inversion: a consumer who can access their own data changes the information asymmetry without banning personalisation itself.

Tradeoff: Implementation costs for retailers. Risk of low consumer engagement. The value accrues primarily to engaged consumers; disengaged consumers remain vulnerable to information asymmetry.

CDR extensionConsumer portabilityACCC oversight
Policy 03 · Competition

Algorithmic Pricing Oversight for ACCC

Equip the ACCC with the mandate and technical capability to monitor algorithmic pricing conduct and personalised promotional practices in real time. The Supermarkets Inquiry showed price signals were already hard to interpret; AI makes this structural.

An outcomes-based approach — the Productivity Commission's (2025) preferred model — monitors competitive conduct and consumer outcomes rather than attempting to ban specific technologies.

Tradeoff: Regulatory capacity is hard to build; algorithmic auditing is a nascent field. Risk of regulatory capture or false negatives. The alternative — no oversight — is worse.

Limitations & What Could Make This Wrong

McKinsey productivity figures are global estimates and may overstate gains in Australia's specific market context. Scenario index values are directional illustrations, not precise projections. Three structural breaks could invalidate the forecast: faster-than-expected AI capability diffusion; a regulatory response stronger than assumed; or distributional effects that diverge significantly from historical technology transitions. These uncertainties are the forecast's most important boundary conditions — not its weaknesses.

Conclusion

The Checkout Is a Choice

By 2030, the most powerful force in Australian retail will be invisible — running in forecast models, loyalty algorithms and dynamic rosters that neither customers nor most workers will ever directly interact with.

The visible store may feel familiar. The infrastructure behind it will not. The prediction rent is real, it is growing, and its distribution is not determined by the technology — it is determined by the policy choices Australia makes before the structural advantage becomes self-reinforcing and permanent. The three interventions identified in this forecast — a retail AI skills compact, extension of the Consumer Data Right to retail, and algorithmic pricing oversight for the ACCC — are not radical proposals. They are the minimum institutional response required to keep the upside scenario in reach.

The ACCC (2025a), Productivity Commission (2025), and JSA (2025) each identify specific mechanisms through which AI adoption in retail could either broaden or concentrate its benefits. The policy levers exist. The question is timing: structural data advantages in AI compound quickly, and regulatory catch-up in data-rich markets has historically been slow. ACCC 2025a; Productivity Commission 2025; JSA 2025.

There is no price of milk anymore — not in the sense Hayek meant, a shared observable signal available to everyone equally. The checkout of 2030 is invisible, personalised, and already forming. The question is only whether Australia shapes it, or inherits it.
